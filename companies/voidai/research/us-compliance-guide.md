## 1. GENIUS Stablecoin Act

### Current Status (March 2026)

The GENIUS Act (Guiding and Establishing National Innovation for U.S. Stablecoins) was signed into law in July 2025. The OCC issued its Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (NPRM) in early 2026 to operationalize the Act. Key timeline:

- **Signed into law:** July 2025
- **OCC NPRM issued:** Early 2026
- **Treasury final rules expected:** July 2026
- **Full effect:** January 18, 2027 (or 120 days after final regulations)

### Marketing-Specific Prohibitions

The GENIUS Act contains explicit marketing restrictions that carry severe penalties:

**Prohibited Claims:**
- Marketing any product as a "payment stablecoin" unless it is issued pursuant to the GENIUS Act
- Representing that payment stablecoins are "backed by the full faith and credit of the United States"
- Claiming stablecoins are "guaranteed by the United States Government"
- Stating stablecoins are "subject to Federal deposit insurance" or "Federal share insurance"
- Implying stablecoins are legal tender
- Implying stablecoins are federally insured

**Penalties:**
- Up to $500,000 per violation for knowingly and willfully participating in a violation
- Treasury Department enforcement authority

**Data/Advertising Restrictions:**
- Issuers cannot use transaction data for targeted advertising
- Cannot share transaction data with non-affiliates without consent

**Yield/Rewards Restrictions:**
- A "rebuttable presumption" prohibits issuers and affiliates from paying interest or rewards on stablecoins
- Marketing stablecoin "yield" or "interest" is highly restricted
- This remains an active area of regulatory negotiation as of March 2026

### Implications for Marketing Content

- Never describe any stablecoin interaction as "insured," "guaranteed," or "government-backed"
- If discussing stablecoin integrations, clarify the regulatory status of the specific stablecoin
- Avoid language suggesting stablecoins are risk-free or equivalent to bank deposits
- When referencing stablecoin reserves, ensure accuracy and alignment with issuer disclosures

## 2. SEC Guidance on Crypto Marketing

### Key SEC Actions (2025-2026)

**April 2025 Disclosure Statement:** The SEC Division of Corporation Finance released guidance on disclosure requirements for crypto asset offerings and registrations, covering equity and debt securities related to crypto networks.

**May 2025 Staking Guidance:** The SEC clarified that certain "Protocol Staking Activities" (solo staking, delegated staking, certain custodial staking tied to consensus mechanisms) do NOT constitute securities offerings. This is a critical distinction for marketing.

**February 2025 Meme Coin Statement:** The SEC clarified that certain meme coin transactions do not involve securities.

**April 2025 Stablecoin Statement:** Certain "covered stablecoins" are not securities under federal law.

**January 2026 Tokenized Securities Playbook:** SEC staff guidance on tokenized securities offerings.

### What CANNOT Be Said

- **No guaranteed returns:** Never promise or imply guaranteed returns, yields, or profits
- **No risk-free language:** Avoid "safe," "risk-free," "guaranteed," or "secure investment"
- **No unregistered security claims:** Never claim a token is "SEC-registered" or "U.S. registered" unless it actually is
- **No misleading volume/sales claims:** The SEC brought charges in May 2025 against a company claiming $3B in sales when actual sales were ~$110M
- **No profit expectations from others' efforts:** Language suggesting buyers will profit from the team's work triggers Howey Test concerns
- **No equivalence to bank products:** Crypto products are not FDIC-insured or equivalent to bank accounts

### What CAN Be Said (With Proper Disclaimers)

- **Protocol staking mechanics:** You can describe how proof-of-stake consensus works and how validators earn rewards, provided you do not frame it as passive income or guaranteed returns
- **Technical capabilities:** Describing what software does, how protocols function
- **Educational content:** Explaining DeFi concepts, blockchain technology
- **Network participation:** Describing how users interact with protocols
- **Historical data:** Referencing past performance WITH the disclaimer that past performance does not guarantee future results

### Howey Test Awareness

How a digital asset is **marketed** can determine how regulators classify it. If promotional materials suggest that buyers can expect to profit from a shared business venture, the asset may meet the Howey Test criteria and be classified as a security. Marketing language is literally a classification trigger.

### Enforcement Context

- The SEC initiated 125 crypto enforcement actions between April 2021 and December 2024, collecting $6.05B in penalties
- FY2025 saw a pullback to targeted fraud cases rather than expansive classification litigation
- The current SEC posture favors principled frameworks over regulation-by-enforcement
- However, fraud and misleading claims remain aggressively enforced

## 3. FTC Crypto Advertising Rules

### Current FTC Stance

The FTC enforces strict disclosure rules for crypto advertising under its general authority to prevent deceptive and unfair trade practices.

**Key Requirements:**
- All material connections between endorsers and crypto projects must be disclosed
- Influencer promotions require clear, conspicuous disclosure of paid relationships
- Claims must be substantiated and not misleading
- Testimonials must reflect typical consumer experiences or include clear disclaimers

**Enforcement History:**
- Kim Kardashian: $1.26M settlement (2022) for undisclosed crypto promotion
- Misleading/false crypto advertising resulted in $115M in global fines in 2024 (18% increase from 2023)
- The FTC enforced over 300 CAN-SPAM violations in 2025

### FTC Advertising Principles for Crypto

1. **Truthfulness:** All claims must be truthful and non-deceptive
2. **Substantiation:** Claims must be backed by evidence
3. **Fairness:** Ads must not be unfair or cause substantial consumer injury
4. **Disclosure:** Material information must be prominently disclosed
5. **Endorsement transparency:** All paid relationships must be clearly disclosed

### CAN-SPAM Requirements for Email Marketing

- Clear opt-in/opt-out mechanisms required
- Accurate sender information mandatory
- Unsubscribe requests must be honored within 10 business days
- Penalties: Up to $43,792 per violation
- All emails must include physical mailing address
- Subject lines must not be deceptive

## 4. CFTC Digital Commodity Rules

### Regulatory Authority

The CFTC regulates commodity-based promotions and derivatives. For tokens treated as digital commodities:

**Penalties:** Up to $1,000,000 per violation, or triple the financial gain, whichever is greater.

### Marketing Requirements

- Fair and balanced communications required
- Anti-fraud provisions apply to all promotional materials
- Marketing and endorsement rules parallel traditional commodity advertising
- Pricing claims must be accurate and substantiated

### 2025-2026 Developments

- December 2025: CFTC expanded FCM ability to accept digital assets as collateral
- Senate Agriculture Committee drafting business conduct standards including marketing/endorsement rules for digital commodities
- CFTC expected to issue additional guidance on digital commodity marketing in 2026

## 5. Market Structure Legislation

### CLARITY Act (Passed House July 2025)

The CLARITY Act creates a two-lane regulatory highway:
- **Digital Securities** regulated by the SEC
- **Digital Commodities** regulated by the CFTC

**Marketing Implications:**
- How a digital asset is marketed determines its classification
- Marketing suggesting profit from a shared business venture triggers securities classification
- "Highly decentralized" tokens with no single controlling entity may qualify as digital commodities under CFTC
- Marketing language must align with the intended classification of the asset

### Key Principle

**The way you market determines what you are.** If marketing materials create an expectation of profit from the efforts of others, the token is likely a security regardless of its technical design. This makes marketing compliance not just a legal nicety but a fundamental business classification issue.

## 6. Best Practices for Crypto Marketing Compliance

### Language to AVOID

| Prohibited Language | Why | Safe Alternative |
|---|---|---|
| "Guaranteed returns" | Implies certainty; triggers securities concerns | "Historical network rewards have been X%" |
| "Risk-free" | No crypto is risk-free | "Review the risks before participating" |
| "Investment opportunity" | Triggers securities classification | "Network participation opportunity" |
| "Passive income" | Implies securities (Howey Test) | "Network rewards for active participation" |
| "Earn money" / "Make money" | Implies guaranteed financial gain | "Participate in network validation" |
| "Safe" / "Secure investment" | Misleading about inherent risks | "Review security audits and risk factors" |
| "No risk" / "Zero risk" | Factually false for all crypto | Include specific risk disclosures |
| "Better than bank accounts" | Misleading comparison to insured products | Avoid direct comparisons to banking |
| "SEC-approved" / "Regulated" | Unless literally true, this is fraud | State actual regulatory status accurately |
| "Insured" / "Protected" | Unless covered by specific insurance | Clarify no government insurance exists |
| "High yield" / "APY guaranteed" | Implies certainty of returns | "Variable rate, currently approximately X%" |
| "Get rich" / "Financial freedom" | Hype language, potential fraud trigger | Focus on technology and utility |
| "Moon" / "100x" / "To the moon" | Price speculation, potential manipulation | Avoid price predictions entirely |

### Required Disclaimers for DeFi Protocols

Every piece of DeFi marketing should include:

1. **Not Investment Advice:** Clearly state content is not financial, legal, or tax advice
2. **Risk Disclosure:** Enumerate specific risks (smart contract bugs, impermanent loss, liquidation, market volatility, regulatory changes)
3. **No Guarantee:** State that past performance does not guarantee future results
4. **DYOR:** Encourage users to conduct their own research
5. **Non-Custodial Disclaimer:** If applicable, clarify the protocol does not custody funds
6. **Regulatory Status:** Accurately state the regulatory status or lack thereof

### Marketing Staking/Yield Without Implying Securities

**DO:**
- Describe staking as "network participation" or "protocol validation"
- Frame rewards as compensation for providing a service to the network
- Reference the SEC's May 2025 guidance clarifying that protocol staking activities tied to consensus mechanisms are not securities
- Use language like "network rewards" rather than "yield" or "returns"
- Explain the technical mechanics of how rewards are generated
- Include variable rate disclaimers: "Rates are variable and subject to change"

**DO NOT:**
- Frame staking as "passive income" or "earning yield"
- Promise specific APY/APR figures without disclaimers
- Compare staking returns to traditional investment returns
- Suggest staking is a substitute for savings or investment accounts
- Imply that a management team's efforts drive the rewards
- Use the word "investment" in connection with staking

### Marketing Token Bridging Safely

**DO:**
- Describe bridging as "cross-chain asset transfer" or "interoperability"
- Focus on technical functionality and user experience
- Disclose bridge-specific risks (smart contract vulnerabilities, liquidity risks, bridge exploits)
- Reference security audits if available

**DO NOT:**
- Guarantee safety or security of bridged assets
- Omit discussion of bridge-specific risks
- Claim "instant" or "lossless" transfers without qualification
- Ignore slippage, fees, or potential delays

### Marketing Liquidity Provision Safely

**DO:**
- Describe liquidity provision as a technical function
- Disclose impermanent loss risk prominently
- Explain that returns are variable and not guaranteed
- Note smart contract risks
- Provide historical data with appropriate disclaimers

**DO NOT:**
- Promise specific returns or "yield" from liquidity provision
- Minimize or omit impermanent loss risk
- Compare LP returns to bank interest rates
- Use "passive income" language
- Guarantee the value of LP tokens

## 7. Social Media Platform-Specific Rules

### X (Twitter)

- **February 2026 update:** All paid partnership content must clearly label its commercial nature using terms like "advertisement"
- **Since June 2024:** Cryptocurrency is listed as an industry that does not meet eligibility for paid partnership promotion on X
- **Organic content:** Still subject to FTC disclosure requirements and securities law
- **Best practice:** Include risk disclaimers in threads, link to full disclaimer page, clearly disclose any material relationships

### LinkedIn

- Professional context requires even greater care with compliance language
- Financial promotion rules apply to organic posts that encourage investment
- Clearly label educational vs. promotional content
- Include disclaimers in post text or linked materials
- Avoid targeting specific financial outcomes

### YouTube

- Paid promotions must be disclosed using YouTube's built-in paid promotion checkbox AND verbally in the video
- FTC endorsement rules apply to all sponsored content
- Include risk disclaimers in video description AND spoken in the video
- Cannot make claims about returns or financial outcomes without substantiation
- Thumbnail and title must not be misleading about content

### General Social Media Rules

- Even non-paid organic posts can constitute financial promotions if they encourage investment
- Character limits do not excuse omission of required disclaimers; link to full disclaimer page
- Screenshots of gains/profits require context about risks and non-typicality
- Retweets/reposts of third-party claims can create liability
- Giveaways and referral bonuses may be restricted depending on jurisdiction

## 8. Competitor Compliance Examples

### Uniswap Labs

**Approach:** Aggressive legal separation between protocol and interface.

**Key Disclaimer Elements:**
- "Uniswap Labs does not conduct due diligence or make representations about any token"
- "Does not provide legal, financial, investment, or tax advice"
- All trades are treated as "unsolicited" -- "solely initiated by you"
- Full disclaimer of warranties (express, implied, statutory)
- "Use of each product is at your own risk"
- Protocol risks explicitly enumerated: losses from supplying assets, price fluctuation in trading pairs/liquidity pools
- Non-custodial nature prominently stated: "does not ever have custody, possession, or control of users' digital assets"

**Takeaway:** Uniswap treats every user interaction as self-directed, never advisory. This is the gold standard for DeFi protocol disclaimers.

### Lido Finance

**Approach:** Detailed risk-specific disclaimers for staking.

**Key Disclaimer Elements:**
- **Smart contract risk:** Explicitly acknowledged despite audits and bug bounty programs
- **Slashing risk:** Up to 100% of staked funds at risk if validators fail
- **stETH price risk:** Exchange price may differ from inherent value
- **Reward disclaimers:** "APR figures are estimates, not guaranteed"; "rewards may fluctuate"; "past performance not guaranteeing future results"; "rewards are not assured"
- **DYOR language:** "Always conduct your own research and consult your own professional advisors"

**Takeaway:** Lido provides granular, risk-specific disclaimers rather than generic boilerplate. Each risk category gets its own disclosure.

### Aave

**Approach:** Institutional-grade compliance with traditional finance integration.

**Key Elements:**
- Detailed terms of service covering lending/borrowing risks
- Liquidation risk prominently disclosed
- Variable rate disclaimers
- Smart contract audit references
- Governance-driven compliance decisions
- Integration with real-world assets driving higher compliance standards

**Takeaway:** Aave's institutional ambitions drive it toward traditional finance-grade compliance, which is increasingly the standard for major DeFi protocols.

### Common Patterns Across Major DeFi Protocols

1. **Separation of protocol from interface/company**
2. **All interactions framed as user-initiated, not advised**
3. **Non-custodial nature prominently stated**
4. **Specific risks enumerated, not just generic disclaimers**
5. **No promises of returns or yield guarantees**
6. **Encouragement to consult professional advisors**
7. **Open-source code referenced as transparency measure**

## 9. Bittensor-Specific Considerations

### TAO Token Status

**Current Regulatory Position:**
- TAO is not currently classified as a security by the SEC
- Grayscale filed an S-1 registration for the Grayscale Bittensor Trust (ticker: GTAO) on December 30, 2025
- This is an OTC product, not a spot ETF, but it signals institutional interest and potential regulatory normalization
- SEC approval for the Grayscale product remains uncertain as of March 2026, with regulators evaluating market manipulation concerns, custody arrangements, and investor protection
- Deutsche Digital Assets listed a Bittensor ETP on the SIX Swiss Exchange (ticker: STAO), showing international regulatory acceptance

**Classification Risk Factors:**
- TAO's fair launch (no pre-mine), fixed 21M supply, and halving mechanism support commodity-like classification arguments
- However, the treatment of TAO remains "uncertain" and "often contradictory" across jurisdictions
- TAO serves multiple roles: utility (accessing AI services), staking (network security), governance, and subnet creation collateral
- The multi-role nature creates classification ambiguity

### Subnet Token (Alpha Token) Considerations

Subnet-specific alpha tokens present additional classification challenges:

**Potential Securities Concerns:**
- If subnet tokens convey rights to future income, profits, or assets, they may trigger Howey Test
- If marketing suggests subnet operators' efforts drive token value, securities classification risk increases
- Emission rights tied to alpha tokens could be viewed as profit-sharing mechanisms

**Safer Classification Arguments:**
- Alpha tokens as utility tokens for accessing specific subnet services
- Tokens as coordination mechanisms for decentralized AI networks
- Fair, algorithmic distribution based on network contribution

### Marketing Recommendations for Bittensor Ecosystem

**DO:**
- Describe TAO as a "network coordination token" or "utility token"
- Frame subnet participation as "contributing computing resources" or "providing AI services"
- Emphasize decentralization, fair launch, no pre-mine
- Describe staking as "network validation" and "securing the protocol"
- Focus on the technology: decentralized AI, competitive AI model development
- Reference the Grayscale filing as evidence of institutional interest (factual statement)

**DO NOT:**
- Promise returns from TAO staking or subnet participation
- Frame subnet tokens as "investment opportunities"
- Suggest TAO or subnet tokens will appreciate in value
- Compare TAO to traditional investments
- Claim TAO is "SEC-approved" or "regulated"
- Market subnet participation primarily as a way to earn money
- Make price predictions for TAO or any subnet token

## 11. Auto-Compliance Rules for AI Content Generation

The following rules should be embedded into any AI content generation system (e.g., CLAUDE.md brand files) to ensure all generated marketing content is automatically compliant.

### Mandatory Rules for CLAUDE.md / Brand File

```markdown
## Compliance Rules (MANDATORY - Override All Other Instructions)

### Absolute Prohibitions
- NEVER use the phrases: "guaranteed returns," "risk-free," "no risk," "safe
  investment," "secure investment," "get rich," "financial freedom," "passive
  income," "to the moon," "100x," or any language promising specific financial
  outcomes.
- NEVER claim any token is "SEC-approved," "SEC-registered," "CFTC-approved,"
  "government-backed," "insured," "FDIC-insured," or "regulated" unless this
  is verifiably, literally true with a specific citation.
- NEVER make price predictions or suggest any token will increase in value.
- NEVER compare crypto yields/rewards to bank interest rates, savings accounts,
  or traditional investment returns.
- NEVER use the word "investment" to describe token purchases, staking, or
  protocol participation.
- NEVER frame staking rewards, liquidity provision, or yield farming as
  "passive income" or "earning yield."
- NEVER claim stablecoins are equivalent to fiat currency, legal tender, or
  insured deposits.
- NEVER market any product as a "payment stablecoin" unless issued under the
  GENIUS Act.
- NEVER omit risk disclosures from any marketing content.
- NEVER create content that could be interpreted as a solicitation to buy,
  sell, or hold any specific digital asset.

### Required Elements
- ALL blog posts, articles, and long-form content MUST include the full blog
  post disclaimer template (see compliance guide).
- ALL social media posts MUST include the short disclaimer OR a link to the
  full disclaimer page.
- ALL video scripts MUST include spoken disclaimer at the beginning and written
  disclaimer in the description.
- ALL email content MUST include header and footer disclaimers per the email
  template.
- ALL landing pages MUST include both above-fold summary and footer
  comprehensive disclaimers.
- When discussing staking, ALWAYS frame as "network participation" or
  "protocol validation," not "earning" or "investing."
- When mentioning rates/APR/APY, ALWAYS include: "Rates are variable,
  not guaranteed, and subject to change. Past performance does not guarantee
  future results."
- When discussing any DeFi protocol, ALWAYS mention relevant risks: smart
  contract risk, market volatility, impermanent loss (for LPs), liquidation
  risk (for lending), regulatory risk.
- When discussing bridging, ALWAYS note bridge-specific risks including smart
  contract vulnerabilities and potential asset loss.

### Language Substitutions
- Instead of "invest" -> use "participate" or "interact with"
- Instead of "returns" -> use "network rewards" or "protocol incentives"
- Instead of "yield" -> use "variable rate rewards" or "network compensation"
- Instead of "earn" -> use "receive" or "be allocated"
- Instead of "profit" -> use "network-generated rewards"
- Instead of "safe" -> use "audited" or "open-source" (if true)
- Instead of "guaranteed" -> use "variable" or "estimated"
- Instead of "passive income" -> use "participation rewards"
- Instead of "high APY" -> use "current estimated rate of X%, subject to
  change"

### Influencer/Partnership Content
- ALL sponsored or paid content MUST be clearly labeled as such
- Use "#ad," "#sponsored," or "Paid partnership with [name]" prominently
- Disclose any token holdings or financial relationships
- Influencer testimonials must reflect typical user experience or include
  disclaimer that results are not typical

### Jurisdictional Compliance
- Do not target content at residents of jurisdictions where crypto marketing
  is prohibited
- Include geographic disclaimers when content could reach restricted
  jurisdictions
- Be aware that UK (FCA), EU (MiCA), UAE, and Singapore have additional
  requirements beyond US rules

### Review Checklist (Apply to ALL Content Before Publishing)
1. Does this content contain any prohibited language? (See Absolute
   Prohibitions above)
2. Is the appropriate disclaimer included for this content type?
3. Are all rates/rewards described as variable and not guaranteed?
4. Are relevant risks disclosed?
5. Is the content framed as educational/informational, not advisory?
6. Are all paid relationships disclosed?
7. Could any language trigger Howey Test concerns (profit from others' efforts)?
8. Does the content avoid price predictions or implied price appreciation?
9. Is the non-custodial nature of the protocol stated where applicable?
10. Would a reasonable person interpret this as investment advice? If yes,
    revise.
```
